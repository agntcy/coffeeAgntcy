# Coffee Agntcy — the business story

**Coffee Agntcy** is a fictitious coffee company used as a shared setting across several demos. Think of it as a
small-to-mid-sized player in the real coffee industry: it **sources** green beans from many places, **trades** and
**contracts** supply, **moves** coffee and paperwork through partners, and **depends on a wider ecosystem** of farms,
logistics, finance, and tools—not everything happens inside one building or one team.

---

## What kind of company this is

Coffee Agntcy sits in the middle of a familiar supply chain:

- **Upstream:** coffee **farms** in different countries, each with different harvests, quality, and availability.
- **Midstream:** **buying** and **allocating** lots (who gets which beans, at what price, under what conditions).
- **Downstream:** **fulfilling customer orders**—reserving stock, arranging **shipment**, and making sure **money and
  records** line up with what was promised.

The demos do not try to simulate every detail of a real roastery or exporter. They pick **recognizable moments** in that
chain so the story stays intuitive even if you have never worked in coffee.

---

## Chapter 1 — Buying and competing for supply

Before you can roast or pack anything, you need **green coffee on contract**.

In this world, Coffee Agntcy does not own every farm. Instead, it **talks to several regional suppliers** and compares
what they can offer: availability, grade, price windows, and sometimes external signals (for example whether **weather**
might affect the next harvest or shipment quality). When a lot looks serious, **payment and commercial terms** matter as
much as taste notes.

So the “buying” chapter is really about **sourcing decisions under uncertainty**: ask the market, compare answers, and
narrow down to a deal you can defend internally.

---

## Chapter 2 — Fulfilling an order end to end

Once coffee is bought—or already in stock—a different drama appears: **someone placed an order**.

Fulfillment is cross-functional by nature. A **farm or warehouse** voice knows what is actually on hand. A **shipper**
cares about lanes, dates, and breakage. An **accountant** (or finance counterpart) cares about invoices, margins, and
whether what shipped matches what was billed. None of those concerns is “the whole order”; they have to **line up** or
the customer gets the wrong coffee, the wrong bill, or the wrong promise.

So the “fulfillment” chapter is about **coordination across specialties** when the company is already mid-flight on a
customer commitment.

---

## Chapter 3 — Finding the right partner or capability

Coffee companies rarely run every capability themselves. Sometimes the question is not “what do we already have?” but
“**who or what in the network can do this for us?**”

That might mean discovering a **new farm**, a **new logistics partner**, a **tool**, or a **service** that was not part
of yesterday’s playbook. The business goal is still mundane: **shorten the distance between intent and a trustworthy
match**, without pretending the directory is always perfect.

So the “discovery” chapter is about **exploring the ecosystem** the company relies on—not mysticism, just the normal
messy work of procurement and partnerships.

---

## The thread that connects the chapters

All three chapters are the **same company** at different moments of the same value chain:

1. **Securing beans** (buying and competition for supply),
2. **Delivering on promises** (operations, logistics, and money),
3. **Extending the network** (finding the right partner or capability when the old list is not enough).

If you remember only one sentence: **Coffee Agntcy is the story of running a coffee business where sourcing, execution,
and partnerships all have to work together—not in isolation.**
