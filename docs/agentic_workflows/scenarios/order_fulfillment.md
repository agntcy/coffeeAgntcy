# Order fulfillment (scenario)

**Order fulfillment** is the chapter of Coffee Agntcy where the company must **deliver on a promise that already left
the sales desk**—coffee has a name, a quantity, a date, and a customer expectation. The scene is a **logistics war
room**: people who normally never share one sentence still have to **align on facts** before money and freight move.

---

## Goal

The operations lead wants to **turn a live order into a coherent execution plan**:

- **Keep inventory honest** — what the **Tatooine estate** can actually release, when pallets exist, and what “ready”
  really means on the ground.
- **Keep freight honest** — what the **shipper** can move under real lane constraints, cutoffs, and breakage risk—not a
  calendar fantasy.
- **Keep money honest** — what the **accountant** will recognize as billable, margin-safe, and reconcilable once the
  shipment exists on paper.
- **Keep the customer promise intact** — nobody gets a heroic story that another function cannot cash.

The episode ends where fulfillment usually ends: **a single story of stock, movement, and settlement** that could be
read back to a buyer without embarrassment.

---

## Who is around the table

|            Voice             |                                                              What they hold                                                               |
| ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Buyer logistics lead**     | The customer-facing promise translated into operational asks: dates, splits, substitutions, and what “done” looks like on arrival.        |
| **Logistics group room**     | The shared air where those asks stop being private side-chats and become **one visible thread** everyone can correct.                     |
| **Tatooine estate (supply)** | Ground truth on what can leave the farm or hand-off point: lots, condition, and the small lies inventory systems tell when nobody checks. |
| **Shipper (movement)**       | Carriage reality: bookings, dwell, weather at sea or road, and the cost of changing a lane after optimism.                                |
| **Accountant (money)**       | Invoice shape, payment timing, and whether the commercial story still matches the margin after freight and fees land.                     |

No single function owns the order. The lead’s job is to **keep the room from narrating three different orders** at the
same time.

---

## How the scene tends to unfold

1. **Open with the promise** — what was sold, to whom, by when, and what flexibility exists if the world disagrees.
2. **Bring supply and movement into the same breath** — the estate speaks to **availability and release**; the shipper
   answers **how it moves**; both feed the same operational picture instead of two private legends.
3. **Let traffic clear through one lane** — updates, reversals, and corrections cross a **single clearing path** so
   nobody optimizes their slice against a version of truth the others never saw.
4. **Pull finance in before heroics** — once movement hardens, the accountant tests whether the plan still **pays** and
   **posts** cleanly; surprises move upstream, not into the customer’s inbox.
5. **Close the beat** — ship as planned, re-cut the plan honestly, or stop the clock with a visible exception—without
   pretending sourcing drama from last week has already been re-litigated here.

That rhythm is what people mean by **order fulfillment**: **cross-functional alignment** under a customer deadline, not
a solo victory lap.

---

## What this chapter is not

- **Not green buying** — origins and auction desks already had their chapter; here coffee is **already chosen** in
  principle.
- **Not marketing** — brand voice and café menus do not decide whether a pallet exists on Tuesday.
- **Not endless discovery** — the cast is **known functions** with conflicting truths, not a blank search for “who sells
  logistics on Earth.”

---

## Why the scene feels tight

- **Inventory and freight disagree politely** until they do not—then someone’s calendar breaks for real.
- **Finance is not a postscript** — the moment someone “just ships it,” margin dies in silence.
- **Silence is expensive** — if the room stops speaking in one thread, three departments will ship three different
  realities.

If that pressure feels ordinary, the scenario is grounded: **operations**, not theatre.

---

## You are still inside order fulfillment when

- A **customer commitment** already exists,
- **Tatooine supply**, **shipping**, and **accounting** must **match** before the door closes,
- The drama is **alignment and exception**, not “which origin had the prettier offer,”
- The beat ends on **execute, reschedule, or escalate**—not on roasting philosophy.
