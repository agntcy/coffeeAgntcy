# Capability discovery (scenario)

**Capability discovery** is the chapter of Coffee Agntcy where the company asks a quieter question: **who or what in the
wider network can actually do the job we need next?** The scene is a **talent and tools counter**—not hiring poetry, not
slide decks: **short, verifiable signal** about real agents, services, and interfaces the firm might rely on tomorrow.

---

## Goal

The recruiting desk wants to **turn a vague need into a short, defensible shortlist**:

- **Read the market of capabilities** — what is registered, under which names, with which interfaces and limits.
- **Filter with prejudice** — hard constraints first (region, compliance posture, throughput), soft preferences second.
- **Explain choices** — why three options beat ten, and what would falsify each pick quickly.
- **Stop before pretend-employment** — discovery ends at **credible matches**, not at signing contracts nobody has read.

The episode ends where good procurement ends: **enough signal to invite deeper diligence or to decline politely**, not a
catalog dump nobody will trust.

---

## Who is around the table

|             Voice              |                                                       What they hold                                                       |
| ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Agentic recruiter**          | The question, the ranking logic, and the obligation to say “no” loudly enough when nothing fits.                           |
| **Agent directory (registry)** | Canonical listings: identities, capabilities, endpoints, and the boring metadata that separates a real offer from a rumor. |

The directory is not the decider; the recruiter is not the registry. **Listings flow into judgment**—that separation
keeps the scene honest.

---

## How the scene tends to unfold

1. **Name the gap** — what capability is missing, in what shape, under what constraints (time, risk, budget band).
2. **Query the shelf** — pull structured answers from the **directory** so the room starts from facts, not memory.
3. **Winnow fast** — eliminate obvious mismatches before anyone falls in love with a clever name.
4. **Stress-test survivors** — ask the questions that expose hollow cards: scope limits, failure modes, operational
  ownership at 2 a.m.
5. **Close the beat** — promote a handful to **deeper evaluation**, park some as “watch,” kill the rest with reasons—
   without pretending the roster is infinite.

That rhythm is what people mean by **capability discovery**: **search with adult supervision**, not a talent show.

---

## What this chapter is not

- **Not fulfillment** — trucks and invoices wait until something is actually bought and moved under a customer promise.
- **Not auction buying** — you are not comparing harvest offers; you are comparing **who can plug a hole** in
  capability.
- **Not open-ended research** — the directory is a **bounded market**, not the entire internet.

---

## Why the scene feels tight

- **Directories lie by omission** — stale cards, optimistic tags, and missing failure notes are normal hazards.
- **Ranking is political** — favorites creep in unless the recruiter keeps the criteria visible.
- **Discovery loves false precision** — ten “maybes” feel productive; three “probablys” with reasons move the company.

If those pressures feel familiar, the scenario is grounded: **procurement of capability**, not mysticism.

---

## You are still inside capability discovery when

- The question starts with **who can do X for us, credibly, soon**,
- A **registry or marketplace of agents** is the honest starting point,
- The beat ends on **shortlist, defer, or decline**—not on running the whole business through a new vendor tonight.
