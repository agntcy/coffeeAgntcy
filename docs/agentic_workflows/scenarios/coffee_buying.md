# Coffee buying (scenario)

**Coffee buying** is the chapter of Coffee Agntcy where the company tries to **secure green coffee** before roasting,
blending, or packing matter. The scene is a **sourcing floor**: a pressured buying lead must **listen to many origins at
once**, compare what the market is saying, and **deepen diligence** exactly where the next mistake would be expensive.

---

## Goal

The auction desk wants to **turn a live sourcing question into a decision it can defend**:

- **Hear everyone who matters now** — Brazil, Colombia, and Vietnam are not decoration; they stand for **different
  calendars, risk profiles, and offer shapes** that rarely line up neatly.
- **Keep comparisons fair** — the same question has to land the same way on each origin so answers can be weighed side
  by side, not as a chain of private confessions.
- **Escalate precision where the lead tightens** — when Colombia becomes the likely home for the lot, the desk reaches
  for **weather** sense (harvest and freight reality) and **payment** sense (how money would actually move) so romance
  does not outrun margin.
- **Stop at a commitment boundary** — buy, push back for terms, or walk—without pretending downstream roasting schedules
  and customer promises have already been solved.

The episode ends where professional buying usually ends: **enough signal to commit or to refuse**, not the entire future
of the company.

---

## Who is around the table

|           Voice           |                                                         What they hold                                                         |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Auction / buying lead** | The mandate, the clock, and the right to change the question when answers drift.                                               |
| **Brazil farm voice**     | What that origin can offer on volume, timing, and posture—stated in its own commercial language.                               |
| **Colombia farm voice**   | A parallel truth for a different soil and season; often where the desk’s attention concentrates once offers start to separate. |
| **Vietnam farm voice**    | Another credible path when the desk is genuinely shopping, not performing a two-party play.                                    |
| **Weather counsel**       | The outside world: rain, drought, wind, and port seasonality that can erase a “good price” overnight.                          |
| **Payment counsel**       | The hard edge of intent: instruments, settlement rhythm, and what “yes” would cost the treasury.                               |

No farm is Coffee Agntcy. The desk’s job is to **curate tension** between legitimate suppliers and the outside facts
that turn offers into obligations.

---

## How the scene tends to unfold

1. **Open the round** — the lead states the need plainly: band of quality, quantity, arrival window, and where
   substitutes are acceptable.
2. **Fan the question outward** — the same inquiry hits **Brazil, Colombia, and Vietnam** in parallel so the room hears
   a market, not a sequence of whispers.
3. **Watch the lane between desk and farms** — offers, hedges, and corrections keep crossing a **single clearing path**
   so the lead can compare without losing messages in a private maze of side channels.
4. **Tighten on Colombia when the story asks for it** — if Colombia becomes the working hypothesis, the desk pulls
   **weather** and **payment** closer so “probably fine” becomes “we can sign without blushing.”
5. **Close the beat** — commit, re-open terms, or exit—leaving fulfillment, roasting, and customer promises for another
   day.

That rhythm is what people mean by an **auction buying** moment: **competition for scarce supply**, **parallel
soundings**, and **branching due diligence** when a lead becomes real.

---

## What this chapter is not

- **Not fulfillment** — trucks, warehouses, invoices, and “did the customer get the bag” belong to a later chapter once
  ownership and shipment exist.
- **Not green-field partner search** — the desk already has **named farms in play**; the drama is **which offer wins**,
  not existential discovery of who sells coffee on Earth.
- **Not the entire exchange** — cupping rooms, sample chains, and regulatory minutiae can sit off-stage; the story keeps
  only what changes a yes/no on this round.

---

## Why the scene feels tight

- **Offers arrive unevenly** — crisp numbers, soft promises, and hedged language still have to be compared honestly.
- **Time fights certainty** — hesitation loses lots; overconfidence writes checks the roastery cannot cash.
- **Weather and payment are not garnish** — they decide whether “a good story” survives first contact with reality.

If those pressures read as ordinary commerce, the scenario is grounded: **green coffee trading**, not a parlor game.

---

## You are still inside coffee buying when

- The question is **which coffee, from which origin, on what terms, under what risk**,
- **Several origins** are live at once,
- **Colombia** is where the desk often **deepens** weather and payment judgment,
- The beat ends on **commit, negotiate, or walk**—not on roasting branding or café menus.
